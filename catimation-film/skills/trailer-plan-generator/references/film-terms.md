# Film Terms For Trailer Plans

Use this reference only when professional terminology or rationale is needed.

## Hitchcock suspense

Working definition: suspense is created when the audience knows a danger, secret, deadline, or threat that the character does not fully know, then watches ordinary or constrained behavior become tense because of that superior knowledge.

Trailer use:

- reveal the condition early
- put the protagonist inside the condition
- end with a choice or question, not a solved outcome

Source anchors:

- Hitchcock's "bomb under the table" distinction between surprise and suspense is commonly discussed through the Hitchcock and Truffaut interview tradition.
- David Bordwell's film writing also summarizes the principle as audience knowledge exceeding character knowledge.

## Kuleshov effect

Working definition: viewers derive meaning from the relationship between sequential shots, not only from a shot in isolation. A neutral or ambiguous face can appear hungry, grieving, guilty, or afraid depending on the surrounding images.

Trailer use:

- show an intense image
- cut to the protagonist's reaction
- let the audience infer the emotional or moral connection

Source anchors:

- Standard film theory attributes the effect to Lev Kuleshov's montage experiments in the 1910s and 1920s.
- Current film references define it as meaning produced by juxtaposed shots.

## MacGuffin

Working definition: a person, object, event, file, mission, secret, or goal that starts and sustains the plot's pursuit, even if its intrinsic identity is less important than the chase and conflict it creates.

Trailer use:

- introduce it early
- make multiple sides want it
- delay full explanation
- let the chase expose character and world conflict

Source anchors:

- Britannica describes the MacGuffin as a trigger that sets the plot in motion, often introduced in the first act or first half.
- The term is strongly associated with Alfred Hitchcock and screenwriter Angus MacPhail.

## Delayed gratification

Working definition: the trailer repeatedly approaches an answer, payoff, image, reunion, or reveal, then cuts away before satisfaction. The delay increases audience desire to see the complete work.

Trailer use:

- set a question
- nearly answer it
- interrupt with a harder contradiction
- reserve the decisive image or explanation

## Omitted causality

Working definition: the edit removes the direct cause-effect bridge. The audience sees setup and aftermath, then reconstructs the missing event through reaction, damage, silence, or changed behavior.

Trailer use:

- show attack preparation, accusation, confession, door opening, or impact setup
- cut away at the causal moment
- return to consequence or reaction

## Chekhov's gun

Working definition: a narrative principle that important introduced elements should later serve a story purpose; a prominent planted detail should be paid off.

Trailer use:

- plant a specific object, gesture, phrase, wound, rule, or ability
- bury it inside pressure and failure
- bring it back at the end as the release mechanism

Source anchors:

- Britannica defines Chekhov's gun as the principle that every introduced story element should be necessary to the plot.
- The common "loaded rifle" formulation is attributed to Anton Chekhov's advice about not placing a rifle on stage unless it will go off.
